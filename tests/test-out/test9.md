My favourite passage from *Leading Cases in Australian Law*:

> In considering the ratio decidendi of the case, the High Court emphasised that the existence of a duty of care is not to be determined by rigid categories,
> but rather through an evaluative process that takes into account salient features of the relationship between the parties.
> These features include, but are not limited to, the foreseeability of harm, the nature of the risk, the degree of control exercised by the defendant, and the vulnerability of the plaintiff.
> Importantly, the Court rejected the proposition that mere foreseeability is sufficient to establish liability, instead framing it as a necessary but not determinative condition.
> The decision thus reflects a broader shift in Australian negligence law toward a multi-factorial approach grounded in principle rather than formalism.

I like that passage so much, I have to repeat it: 

> In considering the ratio decidendi of the case, the High Court emphasised that the existence of a duty of care is not to be determined by rigid categories, but rather through an evaluative process that takes into account salient features of the relationship between the parties. These features include, but are not limited to, the foreseeability of harm, the nature of the risk, the degree of control exercised by the defendant, and the vulnerability of the plaintiff. Importantly, the Court rejected the proposition that mere foreseeability is sufficient to establish liability, instead framing it as a necessary but not determinative condition. The decision thus reflects a broader shift in Australian negligence law toward a multi-factorial approach grounded in principle rather than formalism.
